Open with the learning problem, not the tool. The session is a working conversation for secondary history teachers: what does historical thinking require, where does generative AI quietly do that thinking for students, and how do we design tasks that keep the productive struggle while using AI where it genuinely helps. Invite participants to open the deck on their own device; note that every interactive element runs locally and collects nothing. The frameworks in this session — C.O.R.E. and H.E.A.R.T. — come from my own work in Theory and Research in Social Education and The Leader.

Movement I · Recognize

The 10-second history class

Generative AI can now produce any of these before the bell finishes ringing. Which unsettles you most?

What got skipped: the sourcing, contextualizing, and corroborating that turn five documents into a defensible claim. The essay is the residue of historical thinking — produced here without any of it.

What got skipped: the social risk of stating a claim and being questioned. A simulated seminar performs the discourse without anyone becoming answerable for a position.

What got skipped: the labor of reading against the grain to build the counterargument yourself. Handed the rebuttal, the student never has to think with the text.

The artifact arrives. The question is whether the thinking ever happened.

Chat: what would tell you the thinking actually occurred?Output is not evidence of learning.
Take 45 seconds for a pick and a chat response. The point is not that students will cheat. It is that the finished artifact — the thing we grade — can be produced with none of the cognitive work it was designed to require. Press toward what we would need to see beyond the artifact: explanation, defense, transfer, ownership.
Same grade, different formation

Two students hand in the same essay

Student A · enters the inquiry

Uses AI to surface more sources, generate a position to argue against, and get feedback — then does the sourcing, weighing, and arguing herself.

Student B · exits the inquiry

Asks AI for the thesis and the evidence, edits the wording, and turns it in. The historical thinking happened somewhere else, to someone else.

Same artifact. Opposite learning. The difference is invisible on the page.

Tap your honest read — nothing leaves your device.The grade can't see the difference. Our design has to.
The crux: AI use is not one behavior. The same tool, on the same task, can be the on-ramp to inquiry or the exit from it. If we can't distinguish them from the artifact, the artifact can no longer be the whole assessment. Take the pulse verbally or in chat — it is a per-device reflection, not a live tally.
The foundational question

The question under the panic

If AI can perform the cognitive labor of historical thinking, what happens to the learning that labor was supposed to produce?

Ban it

Loses the tool students will use anyway — and the teachable moment with it.

Embrace it

Loses the struggle that builds the thinking, behind polished output.

Design for friction

Keep the difficulties that do the teaching; use AI where it genuinely amplifies.

This is not first an integrity problem. It is a learning problem — and a design problem.

Not ban. Not embrace. A principled middle path.C3 Framework context
Name the two reflexes — prohibition and uncritical adoption — and reject both. The middle path is a design stance, not a compromise: decide which difficulties are load-bearing for historical thinking and must not disappear, and where AI removes friction that was never teaching anything.

Constructivist foundations

Why the struggle is the point

Productive struggle is not a design flaw.Dewey · Vygotsky · Piaget · Freire
This is the theoretical spine from my Theory and Research in Social Education article. Four traditions converge: authentic learning emerges from struggle, social interaction, and disequilibrium — not from receiving prepackaged answers. Each lens names a specific thing frictionless AI quietly removes. Keep it tight and let the detail panel carry the depth.
Media theory, lightly

The medium has always shaped knowing

McLuhan: the medium reshapes thought.McLuhan · Ong · Carr
One theory beat, kept light and interactive. Earlier media changed how knowledge circulated; generative AI changes who does the reasoning. McLuhan's "the medium is the message" lands here: the tool does not hand students the past to interpret — it hands them the interpretation. Carr's "shallows" names what convenience costs. This reframes "just use it as a research tool."
Defining the term

Friction is productive disequilibrium

Piaget

Understanding changes when new evidence won't fit old schemas — the discomfort that forces accommodation.

Bjork & Bjork

Desirable difficulties: conditions that feel harder in the moment produce more durable, transferable learning.

Thinking is effortful — and that effort is the mechanism, not the obstacle.

Piaget, 1970 · Bjork & Bjork, 2011 · David et al., 2024Friction = useful difficulty by design.
Define the central term precisely so "friction" doesn't drift into "make it hard." Friction is productive disequilibrium: the effortful reconciling of conflicting evidence that builds durable understanding. Note David et al. (2024): mental effort is genuinely unpleasant — which is exactly why frictionless tools are so tempting and so risky.
The framework

Three difficulties worth protecting

Three learner-facing frictions, one institutional condition.Ask which is most neglected in your AI conversations.
Noetic, rhetorical, and existential name the learner-facing struggles historical inquiry depends on. Infrastructural friction names the conditions — pacing, assessment, devices, policy — that decide whether a teacher can preserve the other three without acting alone. Most school AI talk addresses only the institutional layer (acceptable-use policy) and ignores the three that actually form historians.
The crucial guardrail

Not all difficulty is good

The same task can build historical thinking for one student and block the door for another.

Productive friction

Difficulty that builds the sourcing, weighing, or arguing the learner would not otherwise construct.

Exclusionary friction

Difficulty that blocks access, participation, or demonstration without building any historical thinking — decoding load, language barriers, confusing logistics.

For whom does this difficulty build historical thinking — and for whom does it just block the door?

The framework requires judgment, not a blanket rule.Removing a barrier is not the same as removing the learning.
This slide prevents a romantic defense of struggle. "Keep it hard" can reproduce ableism and linguistic exclusion. The productive/exclusionary distinction keeps the framework honest: protect the difficulty that is the historical thinking; remove the difficulty that merely stands in front of it.
What the tool risks

The paradoxes of frictionless AI

Each paradox lands hardest in a discipline built on evidence.Vosoughi · Buolamwini · Freire · Carr
From section III of my article. These are not generic AI worries — they strike directly at history's core. Misinformation undermines sourcing; bias decides whose past gets told and who gets the simplified version; eroded agency revives Freire's banking model; the shallows trade durable learning for fluent output. Use the detail panel to give the history-class example for whichever the room reacts to.

It pairs with H.E.A.R.T. on the next slide — C.O.R.E. anchors the thinking, H.E.A.R.T. anchors the ethics. Both grew from conversations with social studies educators across the country.
The frameworks

H.E.A.R.T. — the ethical dimension

Click a letter for the history-class move.The acronyms remind us how to treat others — and ourselves.
H.E.A.R.T. adds the ethical and emotional parameters that keep AI integration human. In a discipline that handles painful and contested pasts, honesty, empathy, and accountability are not soft skills — they are disciplinary practice. Note that the acronyms are deliberate: they ask us to engage controversy from a stance of human dignity.
A disciplined habit

Technoskepticism: trade-offs, not verdicts

Not pro- or anti-tech — a habit of asking what a tool changes. "Technology inevitably involves trade-offs."

Postman, 1998 · Krutka, Metzger & Seitz, 2022Postman's five propositions ↗
Technoskepticism is the disposition underneath both frameworks: question who benefits, what biases and hidden costs exist, and how adopting a tool reshapes cognition and relationships. Krutka, Metzger, and Seitz show how social studies standards already frame technology as trade-offs. Use one or two of Postman's questions live on AI in your own building.

The document-based question

Five sources on the causes of the American Revolution. Build and defend an argument about which cause mattered most — sourcing, contextualizing, and corroborating across documents that don't agree.

The classic move history teachers assign — and the one AI handles fastest.

The 10-second route

Paste the prompt, receive a sourced thesis with body paragraphs, polish the wording, submit. The artifact is strong. The sourcing, corroboration, and argument never happened — a correct answer with no one home.

Strong output, zero formation. Invisible at the grade level.

What is produced / what is absent
prompt → finished essay
absent: sourcing · weighing · the claim that's yours

The friction-preserving route

Source three documents cold and stake an initial claim. Then ask AI for the strongest rival interpretation, test it against the documents, defend your thesis in seminar, and revise against a new source the tool never saw. Same tool — opposite formation.

AI enters after the thinking, as a sparring partner, not before it as a ghostwriter.

Think first · AI as sparring partner
1 · source cold, stake a claim
2 · AI rival reading → you contest it
3 · defend in seminar, revise
The tool is identical. The sequence is everything.Friction-first, not friction-free.
Walk all three tabs. The key insight is sequence: put the irreducible thinking before the tool, then let AI sharpen rather than supply. The rival-interpretation move turns the abstract's worry — "AI generates counterarguments to the Federalist Papers" — into an asset: students argue against a strong position instead of receiving one.

Not a ban talk

And where AI genuinely amplifies

Access & scaffolding

Translation, reading-level support, and accessibility so the historical thinking is reachable — not removed.

A sparring partner

Generate the strongest counter-thesis — even to the Federalist Papers — then make students rebut it with evidence.

More evidence, faster

Surface additional sources and perspectives, leaving more time for the weighing that matters.

Feedback on drafts

Low-stakes questioning and revision between conferences with the teacher.

Amplify the weighing. Never outsource it.

Civic reasoning in practice: test ownership, map stakeholders, and weigh trade-offs under pressure in the Informed Action game.

Play Informed Action ↗
The middle path uses AI — deliberately.Reachable, not removed.
Spend real time here so the session is not heard as prohibition — this echoes the "best practices for AI integration" in my blog. The test for "amplify" is whether the student still does the irreducible work: AI may carry a multilingual learner over a decoding barrier so they can argue from evidence, but it must not do the arguing. The sparring-partner use makes counterargument a feature, not a threat.
Movement V · Complicate · equity

Friction is unevenly distributed

The tools arrived everywhere

The support to use them well — training, policy, AI literacy — did not.

Technological redlining

"Struggling" labels route some students to thinner content; default narratives skew Eurocentric — deciding whose past is told and who gets the simplified version.

The fix is targeted

Remove barriers unrelated to historical thinking; keep the struggle that is the historical thinking.

Don't preserve exclusion in the name of rigor.

Equity is a design question, not an afterthought.Buolamwini, 2023; Lee, Moore & Hancock, 2023
Tie this back to the friction test and to the bias paradox. "Technological redlining" (Buolamwini) names how personalization can quietly track students into less rigorous content. The most equitable design often uses AI to clear an access barrier and raises the demand for sourcing, argument, and defense.
The conditions we teach in

Balanced history in a polarized world

Pick a pressure on the history classroom. What's a framework-grounded response?

Response: teach students how to think, not what to think. Multiple sourced perspectives and a transparent method make academic rigor the through-line.

Response: lead with H.E.A.R.T. — honesty and respect for each student's core identity; debate both sides from evidence, in a classroom built on dignity.

Response: slow the fast media. C.O.R.E. respect and structured discourse over reaction; name how feeds curate the conflict in the first place.

Our job: teach students how to think, not what to think.

Practice it: take the teacher's chair in Common Ground — five charged classroom moments, judged by your C.O.R.E. and your H.E.A.R.T.
