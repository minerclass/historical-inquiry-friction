What Contextualization IS

Contextualization is the core move of historical thinking. It requires you to rebuild a foreign worldview, mapping out what people believed, feared, knew, and lacked. It demands that you treat past actions as rational responses to immediate, complex pressures.

When you contextualize, you respect the limits of time. You recognize that the actors had to make decisions in the dark, without the benefit of knowing how the story ends.

What Contextualization is NOT

Contextualization is not moral relativism, nor is it exoneration. Empathizing with the constraints of historical actors does not mean endorsing their decisions or excusing atrocities.

Historians do both: they explain an action using context, and they evaluate the ethical weight and consequences of that action. The danger is merging the two: skipping the explanation to leap directly to immediate hindsight condemnation.

Connected Frameworks

C.O.R.E. Openness

Resist the flat, clean summaries generated by AI that resolve historical conflicts. Openness means entering the historical space as a realm of genuine contestation, multiple competing perspectives, and unresolved tension.

H.E.A.R.T. Empathy

Engage in historical perspective-taking. Empathy means respecting the cognitive and emotional realities of people who lived in contexts radically different from our own, understanding them on their own terms before passing judgment.
